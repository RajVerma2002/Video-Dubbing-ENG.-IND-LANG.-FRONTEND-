IPR FAQs

Intellectual property (IP)

Intellectual property (IP) refers to creations of the mind, including works of art, inventions, computer programs, trademarks, and other commercial signs. It encompasses various legal protections for these creative and innovative works.

Why does intellectual property (IP) matter?

IP matters because it encourages innovation, economic growth, and the protection of creators' and inventors' rights. It plays a crucial role in fostering new ideas, technological progress, and cultural development.

Main types of intellectual property rights?

The main types of intellectual property rights include copyrights, patents, trademarks, industrial designs, and geographical indications. These rights protect different types of creative and innovative works.

How does copyright protect creative works?

Copyright protects literary, artistic, and scientific works, granting creators exclusive rights to control how their works are used. This includes reproduction, performance, broadcasting, and distribution rights.

What can be patented, and how does patent protection work?

Patents protect inventions that offer new solutions to problems. To qualify for patent protection, an invention must be novel, useful, non-obvious, and not fall under non-patentable subject matter. Patents are territorial and grant exclusive rights to make, sell, and use the patented invention.

What are trademarks, and why are they important?

Trademarks are signs that distinguish the goods or services of one enterprise from others. They are crucial for branding, as they help businesses protect their reputation and consumers identify genuine products and services.

How does international law contribute to intellectual property protection?

International law, administered by organizations like WIPO, establishes minimum standards of protection for intellectual property across countries. It facilitates global cooperation, negotiations for new rules, and the protection of IP rights beyond national borders.

What is WIPO's role in intellectual property?

WIPO, the World Intellectual Property Organization, plays a vital role in shaping international IP rules, delivering global IP services, aiding development through IP, and providing IP-related information and infrastructure.

How do intellectual property rights balance creator and public interests?

P rights strike a balance by granting creators exclusive rights for innovation and creativity while also allowing exceptions for public access and use when necessary.
